## 1. Origin Story

### The EverQuest Foundation

Jeff Kaplan was "Tigole" -- guild leader of Legacy of Steel on The Nameless server
in EverQuest. His guild had multiple world-first raid kills. His notorious rants on
Sony Online Entertainment's forums about endgame design made him a community
celebrity.

**On EverQuest's impact:**
> "EverQuest, I can describe it as nothing short of magical, what that game did for
> gaming and me personally. I really regard that as one of the greatest games of all
> time."

**On the EQ community's depth:**
> "By creating this small community it really sort of perpetuated and encouraged very
> deep design discussions about the game itself."

**On the collaborative model between players and designers:**
> "I wish that had been done with me when I was a player. I feel like I could have
> helped make some of the games I played better if there had been an open dialogue."

### Getting Hired at Blizzard

Kaplan's guildmate in EverQuest was Rob Pardo, then lead designer on Warcraft III.

> "I got started in the industry almost by accident; I fell into it. I always thought
> you had to be an amazing programmer or a really talented artist to have a career in
> video games."

> "What I didn't know, what I learned later, was that many of my guild members in
> EverQuest worked at Blizzard."

> "Rob in guild chat saying to me, 'Hey, check the job postings on the Blizzard
> website tomorrow.' I swear to you the job description was written exactly to my
> background."

### The Writing Career That Wasn't

Before games, Kaplan tried to be a fiction writer. He received 170 rejection letters.

> "The universe was telling me it was time to give up as a writer."

> "I had structured my life where I was going to write for eight hours a day."

**Key principle:** "Focus on what you want to do, not what you want to be."

## 2. WoW Era: Quest Design

### GDC 2009: "The Cruise Director of Azeroth"

Kaplan's landmark GDC talk laid out his philosophy of "directed gameplay" and
systematically catalogued WoW's quest design failures.

**Core concept -- Directed Gameplay:**
> "A phrase we use at Blizzard to represent the idea of leading a player to a fun
> experience. An underlying tool to help a player become immersed in your game."

> "We wanted Azeroth to feel like an amazing world that players could enter and explore
> and enjoy... But when it came to our content specific to the player being guided
> through our world, we were going to do it all ourselves."

**The prime directive:**
> "Make completing quests the smart way to play the game."

### The Nine Worst Quest Design Problems

**1. The Christmas Tree Effect**
Players arrive at quest hubs where "your minimap is lit up like a Christmas tree with
quests." Designers lose control over guiding players through intentional experiences.

**2. Too Long, Didn't Read**
> "We need to stop writing a fucking book in our game, because nobody wants to read it."

WoW limits quest text to 511 characters. Kaplan wished it were smaller:
> "I think it's great to limit people in how much pure text they can force on the player."

> "Screw the background, just tell people 'Kill 10 foozles and you get a cookie' since
> that's all that matters."

**3. Mystery Misplacement**
> "The mystery should never be in the action that the player needs to do."

> "The mystery can't be what to do. We wanted the action in WoW quests to be in the
> gameplay, not in figuring out what am I supposed to do."

**4. Poorly Paced Quest Chains**
> "We lose trust in the player" when encounters become frustratingly difficult.

> "It's basically like putting a brick wall in front of a player. Here you go, just
> bang your head against the wall."

**5. Gimmick Quests Without Polish**
> "You've played that shooter... it's got seven of the best levels you've ever played
> and then it's got that one vehicle level where they didn't know what they were doing."

> "Lots of these vehicle quests, they're more fun for the designer than they are for
> the player."

> "You need to understand what the core of your game is."

**6. Bad Quest Flow**
Four kill quests followed by four collection quests creates monotonous pacing. Solution:
"Multiple routes to the goal, and if possible, special non-generic (but non-gimmicky)
elements."

**7. Collection Quest Mistakes**
Problems: poor flow, insufficient creature density, too many items required, requiring
overly broad item variety.
> "You never want the player to even think somebody made the game."

**8. Green Hills of Stranglethorn -- The Self-Own**
> "This is the worst quest in World of Warcraft. I made it."

> "It was the typical hubris of a junior game designer who thinks he's clever but is
> actually a dipshit. That's The Green Hills of Stranglethorn summed up."

> "For a single quest to consume 19 spaces in your bags is just ridiculous."

**9. Arbitrary Collection Without Narrative Purpose**
Collecting items feels pointless without story payoff. Solution: "celebration moments"
that make players feel the effort was rewarded.

### Solutions Implemented in Wrath of the Lich King

**Progressive Drop Rate System:** Rather than flat percentage chances creating
frustrating streaks, WotLK implemented progressive scaling -- first kill 16% drop
chance, second 32%, progressing to 100%.

**Breadcrumbing:** Reduced excessive travel distances through the world.

**Play your own content:** Designers must personally play their own content immediately
during implementation.

**Combat timing target:** One-minute fights. Non-combat timing: "gut feel."

### Quest Design Evolution (EQ -> WoW)

> "I came from a very EverQuest mindset which was, you'll have a few quests which will
> take you to an area, then once you're in that area, you'll be excited there are
> creatures to fight, then you'll fight the creatures for a few hours and eventually
> get bored and then look for a new quest after that."

He realized this approach "was not going to be accessible if they wanted to broaden
their reach."

**Scale:** 8,570,222,436 quests completed between June 2007-March 2009 (16 million
daily). Quest count: 2,600 (launch) -> 5,300 (TBC) -> 7,650 (WotLK).

## 3. WoW Era: World & Raid Design

### Zone & World Design

Kaplan was the "medium" between Chris Metzen's creative vision and the
programmer/artist level design team. He translated lore into playable spaces.

**On seamless world design:**
> "On a purely technological point, we wanted to make a seamless world, where when you
> travel from one zone to another, you didn't get a loading screen."

**On wanting first-person WoW:**
> "I really wanted WoW to be first-person. I argued a lot for that."

His preference came from EverQuest, where first-person perspective meant "I saw my
guildmates up close way more and felt like I appreciated the character art way more."

### The Horde/Alliance Split

One of WoW's most consequential design decisions was nearly not made.

> "The Horde/Alliance decision was really controversial. Because in EverQuest it was
> mixed... Rob and I came from EverQuest, where we felt like this was a horrible
> decision Allen [Adham] was making. And we argued."

> "Allen, Bob Fitch and I would have lunch every single day, and we would just talk
> about WoW and the core design of WoW... We would fight over the Horde/Alliance split
> and if it was a good idea or not."

Allen Adham retired 9-12 months before WoW shipped. Rob Pardo took over as lead
designer.

> "To Rob's credit the first thing he did was go: Allen's a smart guy, the fact he was
> fighting so hard for Horde/Alliance? We gotta do it."

Allen Adham was inspired by Dark Age of Camelot: "He liked the fact you were instantly
on a team, you weren't a loner in the world, and whether you liked it or not you had
people on your side."

### Raid & Dungeon Design

**On punishing vs. hardcore:**
> "I think if your game is too punishing, and I like to separate the words 'punishing'
> from 'hardcore,' I think it goes against the nature of trying to create a fun
> experience for people."

**On Karazhan:**
> "That dungeon... was completely -- from the ground up without a single asset saved --
> rebuilt three times. It was a very painful experience."

> "It just felt like there were so many different areas to the dungeon, it had some
> really cool themes, some really cool characters."

**On Auchindoun's over-streamlining:**
> "I do think we made some mistakes in streamlining it... it felt kind of like a linear
> tunnel, and you were just going to fight through."

**On celebrating dungeon completion:**
> "At the end of a dungeon run, I always felt like we could celebrate the moment more...
> you just kind of zone out and it's like a load screen."

### WoW Accessibility Evolution

The biggest design philosophy change between Burning Crusade and Wrath of the Lich King:
> "An eye toward accessibility and trying to get more players to experience more of
> the content."

## 4. Blizzard Core Design Mantras

These were company-wide philosophies that Kaplan worked within. Articulated primarily
by Rob Pardo in GDC/AGC talks, but Kaplan embodied them in practice:

### "Concentrated Coolness"
> "Rather than making as much variety and many different things as you can, make sure
> the things you do make are as cool as possible."

Applied to WoW: 9 distinct classes rather than 20+, ensuring each felt genuinely
unique in mechanics and identity.

### "Easy to Learn, Difficult to Master"
> "The first thing we always do is design the depth first and the accessibility later."

The approach ensures engaging core mechanics exist before simplifying the interface.
Appeals to both casual and hardcore players.

### "Killing with a Purpose"
Quest design philosophy providing narrative reasons for combat beyond XP. Instead of
grinding: "You're always moving around the world, seeing a new place."

### "Purity in Purpose"
Maintaining design integrity by avoiding compromises. Blizzard refused creating solo
dungeons despite requests, preserving group content's prestige rather than diluting
both experiences.

### Solo-ability as Baseline
A core goal for WoW: the game would be solo-able to level 60, making it available
to everyone so players could experience it like a single-player game if they wanted.

## 5. Project Titan: Failure & Lessons

### What Titan Was

> "Project Titan was a multicomplex game where you played a normal life during daylight:
> day job, build, housing, recreation, driving, etc., and at night you would do spy
> agent work like infiltrating, killing, and stuff -- first-person shooter with special
> abilities."

- Cost: $83 million
- Development: ~2007-2013
- Result: Complete cancellation

### Why It Failed

**On leadership responsibility:**
> "It was an engineering failure, an art failure, and a design failure."

> "The hubris of Blizzard."

**On the team being paralyzed:**
> "Out of 40 hours that week, he only worked 20 hours. He was one of the top developers.
> To scale, that means most people had a lot of downtime."

> "Imagine, you are building a team of the best in the industry, and they can't work.
> Not only are you just burning cash faster than anybody on the planet, it is also like
> imagine having fighter pilots but we don't let them fly. The creative frustration and
> how it manifested and demoralized the team. It was a disaster."

**On knowing early it was doomed:**
> "We've got to shut this down; we're just burning money." (circa 2010)

By 2013, Kaplan recognized Titan "could never ship and would never exist" in its
current form.

### The Critical Lesson: Team Size & Validation

**The wrong approach (what Titan did):**
Hired too many people early in development.

**The right approach:**
> "The right way to incubate a video game is to hire a small team and to try to get
> the idea across with whatever technology you can get your hands on and prove out that
> idea. Once you know what that game feels like, then you expand the team."

## 6. Overwatch: Creation from Despair

### The Six-Week Pitch

After Titan's cancellation, the team had six weeks to prove themselves.

> "It was supposed to be the greatest time ever... We were probably the most
> demoralised we'd ever been in our careers."

> "You had a really amazing group that was working on Titan, really talented
> individuals, but we failed horrifically in every way. We failed in every way a
> project can fail. And it was devastating."

> "To go through such a complete and utter failure is very hard for people who are used
> to experiencing success."

**The three pitches:** Overwatch, a StarCraft MMO, and Crossworlds. Two weeks each.

**The hunger to redeem:**
> "What can I do next to prove that that's not who I am?"

> "There was an extremely tight bond on the team and a ravenous hunger to show the
> world that we're not failures and we can make something really fun."

### The Core Insight

Kaplan wanted a game with 50 classes, each very focused -- the opposite of Titan's
muddled design.

> "We really chased our hearts more than anything else."

> "We were looking at what is super great about these games... It's that idea of
> delivering heroes as characters."

**On Team Fortress 2's influence:**
> "Team Fortress 2 not only was a juggernaut in terms of class-based team shooters,
> but I think it transcended game design."

### The FPS Origin

> "Shooters were really my first love when it comes to video games. I think of Doom,
> Duke Nukem, Quake, Wolf 3D; they were really some of my biggest inspirations."

> "One thing that I was never fully in love with in any of the MMOs that I had played
> was the combat itself."

> "Nothing quite spoke to me like playing Quake 3 Arena in terms of action combat."

### Pitching to Bobby Kotick

While showing the hero lineup to Activision CEO Bobby Kotick:
> "I'm thinking 'Oh no, what was back three slides?'" -- when Kotick asked him to go
> back (Kotick was actually interested, not concerned).

### Overwatch as Shared Passion

> "It's unique in the fact that it's a collaborative passion project that's shared by
> the entire team. I've never been on a team that shared such a unified vision."

> "Closing a door is required for another door to open."

## 7. Overwatch: World-Building Philosophy

### "A Future Worth Fighting For"

The team's guiding phrase, repurposed from Titan. A deliberate rejection of gaming's
dystopia obsession.

> "Having a bright welcoming world really attracts a broader audience."

> "We play games to escape, and challenging, oppressive environments tend to fatigue
> people mentally."

They surveyed the landscape -- The Last of Us, Fallout 4, Call of Duty, Battlefield --
and saw bleak futures everywhere. They wanted the opposite.

The phrase justified conflict: it's not a perfect future, but players should understand
why they would want to live in it and why it's worth defending.

### Inclusivity as Core Design

> "Overwatch at its core is about inclusivity and the vast diversity in humanity."

> "A lot of us have different fantasies of who we want to be. And when you have such a
> limited imagination of what those fantasies could be -- for a player to live out in
> their gaming experience -- you're not speaking to as wide of an audience as you can."

> "None of our storylines are about the princess in the castle who needs to be rescued.
> The women in our games are the ones doing the rescuing. They're some of the strongest
> characters that we have. They're not victims -- they're not waiting to be saved by
> a man."

> "Gamers, at a certain point, are genderless... What does the team need to win right
> now? It boils down to that."

On the Oasis map (set in Iraq, founded by six scientists from different nations):
"Let's stop reinforcing the stereotype."

On representation being normalized: Kaplan wrote that "the most important thing to him
is that those in positions to influence society start portraying normal things as being
normal," and he "longed for the day when featuring characters of different backgrounds
wouldn't be newsworthy but instead just normal."

## 8. Hero Design Philosophy

### Characters, Not Classes

> "Not just giving people classes, but really developing heroes. Not only in terms of
> what their gameplay abilities were, but like that Tracer was a test pilot in the RAF."

> "Try to take these characters and not only make them awesome gameplay heroes, but try
> to build characters in a new universe."

> "The biggest difference is that we're focused on creating a game about heroes. Really
> putting that personality into the characters -- I think that was the huge difference."

### Accessibility Through Invitation

> "We try to then say: We know how to create this really fun hardcore loop in a game.
> But how do you open the door and make everyone feel welcome?"

> "You get rid of punishing elements that don't add to the depth."

### Hero Swapping as Problem-Solving (The Design Intent That Failed)

> "We believed that with hero swapping, people would be more willing to adapt to 'solve
> the problem'. We're fighting some amount of human psychology and game design, I know."

> "We hoped to be able to create more highly situational characters with the thought
> that players would switch in situations where those characters weren't as viable."

But "maining" and "one-tricking" became dominant. The team didn't expect this.

### The Offense/Defense Merge Regret

> "Sometimes I wish we had kept offense and defense heroes rather than merging them
> into damage, and only allowing offense heroes to be played on attack, and defense
> to be played on defense."

> "I know that idea might sound crazy and stupid but I think it would have allowed us
> to go more 'all in' on some 'niche' hero designs like Torb, Symmetra and Bastion."

> "Much of the issue we face with the hero balance on these heroes is our player
> expectations that these heroes should be fully viable in all situations, and that
> really wasn't our intent or vision for the game."

### Progression System Iteration

The team went through three progression system designs:

1. **Traditional/expected system** -- "It was just too much to process... It just became
   way overwhelming."
2. **Hero-specific leveling** -- "A terrible mistake because it overly rewarded players
   staying on one hero."
3. **Account-level loot boxes** -- "Instead we're giving you an account player level...
   you, the player, will level up. Each time you hit a level... we will give you what
   we call a loot box."

> "The majority of items on each of the characters... you can just unlock it for credits
> if you want."

## 9. Balance Philosophy

### The Perception Principle

> "The perception of balance is more powerful than balance itself."

> "All heroes are affected by changes to other heroes. Even if nothing changed, players
> would adapt new strategies and tactics that would cause the balance of the game to
> shift."

### Data vs. Feeling

> "Statistically, the things that are most unbalanced aren't what you think they are.
> Symmetra and Torbjorn's win rates are not balanced. They are too good. But this is
> why we don't balance on statistics alone."

### Hold Your Ground (The D.Va Case Study)

> "A few months ago we nerfed D.Va and we faced the ire of many very upset players who
> thought we 'ruined' the hero and she would never be played again."

> "We held our ground because we believed she was fine. We did not touch her. And now
> she is one of the dominant heroes in the dive meta -- clearly not in need of a buff."

### Aggressive Meta Targeting

> "Balance more frequently, more aggressively, and be less concerned with trying
> something and maybe pulling it back later."

> "Deliberately target the meta and move you off the meta as frequently as possible."

### Iterative Confidence

> "We've been a little precious with our balancing... players want newness thrust upon
> them, and we can give them that."

> "In year four we feel like there's a maturity to the audience who's still with us,
> where they want change. They want us to try things out and they're okay if we try
> something out, it doesn't work, us changing it again. It's a very different
> philosophy."

### Meta Innovation

> "It's always fun to study how people innovate to 'break' a meta."

### Matchmaking Reality

> "The matchmaking systems are some of the most complex design engineering tasks you're
> ever going to tackle, and they're thankless. There's gotta be winners and there's
> gotta be losers."

On checking complaining players' accounts:
> "We would study it all the time when people would complain. You see a Reddit post and
> somebody would say 'I had a six game losing streak, this is so fucked, it's the worst
> matchmaker ever'... we would look up that person's account, we'd do that all the time,
> I love looking at people's accounts and seeing what happened."

## 10. Community & Toxicity

### The Developer Update Model

Kaplan made himself the public face of Overwatch, regularly posting to forums and
creating developer update videos. He became a celebrity within the community.

His priority: explaining the vision for the game as clearly as possible, addressing
popular opinion, covering not only changes but also *lack* of changes on things
believed to be overpowered.

### On Being Held to Standards

> "I love being held to high expectations. And part of the thrill of what we do is
> trying to meet them. I know we won't always meet them."

> "All of the nitpicking comes from a place of passion. People who really don't care
> about Overwatch wouldn't be part of the community."

### The Toxicity Problem

> "The community needs to take a deep look inwards, and consider: I'm playing this
> game because I want to have fun, and the other people, the other team, they want to
> have fun as well."

> "Think of all the times someone said something negative to you in the game, and
> imagine if they said something positive instead."

> "Our highest-level philosophy is if you're a bad person doing bad things in Overwatch,
> we don't want you in Overwatch. We don't want to create areas for you where just the
> bad people are in Overwatch."

Of 480,000 players who received disciplinary action, 340,000 were a result of player
reports. Resources for new content (maps, heroes, animated shorts) were "siphoned to
fighting abuse and a rising tide of toxicity."

### On Dehumanization in Online Spaces

> "People are just horrible to each other... It's because we've dehumanised the other
> person and there's anonymity to it."

### The Developer-Community Paradox

> "But the second you go, 'Let's try to be a little bit cooler about what we do with
> the community,' we actually get destroyed by that very community."

### On Critics Who Don't Play

Post-Blizzard (2026), on gamers who trash games they've never played:

> "Shut the fuck up. No one cares. We don't need to hear that you weren't into it."

He called this behavior "nerd baby rage." Acknowledged he's "given it out at times"
himself -- but his EverQuest era rants were "at least motivated by having played the
game in the first place."

> "The closest I come to a midlife crisis is giving zero fucks."

## 11. Regrets & What He'd Do Differently

### Team vs. Individual Focus

> "One of my hindsight regrets about Overwatch, and I think we did the right thing in
> the moment, I wouldn't go back and redo it, but if I was making a hero shooter from
> scratch today, I would make it less team-focused."

> "I would actually downplay the team factor and try to put more focus on individual
> contribution. Because that's just how people play, they're selfish. And I don't mean
> that in a bad way, it's just that human nature that they can't help."

> "We put all of our eggs in you noticing if the team won or lost, and we downplayed
> individual contributions as much as possible. There wasn't a scoreboard, there was a
> medal system -- but the medal system, in my opinion, was not good because the losing
> team got medals and the winning team got medals and the losing team would weaponize
> it against their teammates."

### Overwatch 2

> "Overwatch 2 is out now, but it's not the Overwatch 2 we planned and announced."

> "I believed in Overwatch 2. I think we could have made a great... I have a lot of
> hindsight of how I would have designed that game differently with what I know now
> versus what ultimately we didn't ship."

Kaplan called it "one of my biggest mistakes" -- citing devs on the team who "didn't
like PvP or competitive shooters" pushing for PvE, combined with executive pressure
becoming "monumental."

### Role Queue Solving a Social Problem

> "Role queue automatically did that... we eliminated that conversation ever happening
> again in Overwatch."

On how maturity changes design space:
> "Now Overwatch is four years old, we have to be very careful about the amount of
> dramatic decisions that we make."

> "We had moments where the game was eight versus eight... those are not the types of
> choices we can make anymore."

## 12. Leaving Blizzard: Corporate vs. Creative

### The Breaking Point

The CFO called Kaplan in and set revenue targets for Overwatch with a threat:

> "Overwatch has to make [bleep] in 2020, and then every year after that it needs a
> recurring revenue of [bleep]... if it doesn't do [bleep] we're going to lay off
> 1,000 people, and that's going to be on you."

> "And that was just the biggest 'fuck you' moment I had in my career. It felt surreal
> to be in that condition."

> "I had believed I would never work any place but Blizzard. I loved it. It was a part
> of who I was, and I felt I was a part of it. And I literally thought I would retire
> from the place, I never thought the day would come. And that was it, I was like,
> 'we're done here'."

### The Overwatch League Disaster

> "Overwatch League was the best intention -- my parents always say the road to Hell is
> paved with good intentions, that was the Overwatch League -- and it ended up being
> an albatross."

> "A house of cards." "A great idea with the wrong instincts."

> "There was a lot of focus on 'let's make lots of money really fast', and a lot of
> people got dragged into it."

Leadership was "pretty much selling the Brooklyn Bridge, that Overwatch League was
going to be more popular than the NFL." They projected $125 million in revenue that
never materialized.

### On Recovery

> "Separating from Blizzard was one of the most painful things."

> He didn't realize "how broken" he was "until recently, like the mourning, grieving
> I had gone through."

### On Developers and Value

> "I wish game developers would know their value more and not hand over the golden
> goose to people who don't deserve it."

## 13. Post-Blizzard: The Legend of California

### New Studio: Kintsugiyama (34 developers)

> "I don't want to crib Blizzard and make a pseudo-Blizzard game."

### Fast-Forwarding to Fun

> "What makes this game really unique and special in my opinion is that we sort of
> fast-forward you to the cool stuff as quickly as possible."

> Not "starving the player out and forcing them to wait for all the cool content."

Excitement around "guns, mining, exploration, and horses" -- get players to the good
stuff immediately.

### Building Philosophy

Building is "more built like Valheim and Subnautica, where the building is more focused
on utility, progression, aesthetics, and customization, rather than defending your stuff."

> "I would never want to mislead Rust players into thinking this is cowboy Rust or
> California Rust. We are much more balanced between a PvE and PvP-type game."

### World Resets as Excitement

> "I love the resetting worlds. It's a great mechanic and one I want to evolve and
> work upon."

> "The trick is coming up with not 'why am I upset that the world resets?' But 'why am
> I excited that the world resets?'"

> "We want it to be fast enough that you're not too attached [to your gear], but we
> wanna make it rewarding."

### Art as Foundation

The game is heavily inspired by painter Albert Bierstadt's 19th-century California
landscapes. The goal: creating a beautiful, working, interesting world that players
will want to come back to.

## 14. On AI

> "No matter how good AI gets, it's never going to draw a picture like Arnold Tsang.
> It's never going to tell a story like Chris Metzen. The human spirit is irreplaceable."

> "No one's creative work should be used by AI without their permission. Voice actors,
> artists, it can't be lifting from them without their permission; that's just immoral,
> it's no different than just sort of stealing."

On AI's current state in game dev: "mostly a hot mess" and "an interesting fever dream."
It is "overconfident in what it tries to deliver."

Practical use at Kintsugiyama (34 people): AI for "points of tedium" -- mundane tasks
like batch-fixing incorrectly-sized images. Not for replacing creative workers.

## 15. Meta-Principles: The Jeff Kaplan Design Canon

Distilled from 20+ years of quotes, talks, and decisions:

### On Making Games
1. **Chase your heart.** "We really chased our hearts more than anything else."
2. **Start small, prove the idea.** "Hire a small team and try to get the idea across
   with whatever technology you can get your hands on."
3. **You're only as good as your last patch.** Constant hunger to improve.
4. **Play your own game.** Designers must personally play their content during
   implementation.
5. **Fun is uncertain until proven.** "You never know when you're making something if
   it's gonna be fun, you only believe it's fun."

### On Players
6. **Don't write a book.** Players don't read. Actions > text. 511 characters max.
7. **Clarity of action.** The mystery should never be in what the player needs to do.
8. **Respect player time.** Don't make them wait for the cool stuff. Don't tax
   inventory. Don't punish without purpose.
9. **Perception > reality.** The perception of balance is more powerful than balance
   itself.
10. **Players are selfish (and that's fine).** Design for how people actually play,
    not how you wish they'd play.
11. **Players don't switch.** They main. Design accordingly.
12. **Celebrate moments.** The end of a dungeon, a quest chain payoff -- mark these.

### On Teams & Culture
13. **Unified vision matters.** The best teams share a collaborative passion.
14. **Failure can bond.** Titan's failure created Overwatch's tight-knit team.
15. **Separating "punishing" from "hardcore."** Punishing drives people away.
    Hardcore attracts them. Know the difference.
16. **Bright > grim.** "Having a bright welcoming world really attracts a broader
    audience."

### On Community
17. **Transparency builds trust.** Explain the vision as clearly as possible. Address
    what you're NOT changing and why.
18. **Hold your ground.** When data says the balance is right, don't cave to outcry.
19. **Criticism from passion.** "All of the nitpicking comes from a place of passion."
20. **Toxicity is a development cost.** It directly steals resources from content
    creation.

### On Business
21. **Don't hand the golden goose to people who don't deserve it.** Protect creative
    work from corporate exploitation.
22. **Revenue ultimatums kill games.** The CFO meeting was the beginning of the end.
23. **Overpromising kills leagues.** "Selling the Brooklyn Bridge."
